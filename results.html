Results

Exceptional generalization: GABAR's coverage (success rate) drops minimally as problems get harder: 95.5% on Easy, 92.2% on Medium, and 89.2% on Hard. In contrast, baseline methods like GPL and GRAPL see their coverage collapse to 6.5% and 22.1% on hard problems, respectively.

Solves problems 8x larger than training: In the Visitall domain, the model was trained on problems with 9-36 cells (up to 49 in validation). It successfully solved test problems with up to 400 cells, more than 8 times larger than the largest training instances.

Outperforms modern LLMs: On hard test instances, LLMs like OpenAI-O3 and Gemini-2.5-Pro solve almost no problems (0.4% and 1.5% coverage, respectively). GABAR's architecture, which explicitly learns structural relationships, maintains 89.2% coverage.

Key Findings from Ablations

Action nodes are critical: Removing them (GABAR-ACT) drops coverage from 89.2% to 7.4% on hard problems. This confirms that explicitly modeling action-object relationships is essential.

Conditional decoding matters: Independent parameter selection (GABAR-CD) drops coverage to 60% on hard problems. This failed to capture dependencies (e.g., in Logistics).

Ranking beats value learning: Using a value-learning objective (GABAR-RANK) drops coverage to 12.1% on hard problems, proving that learning to rank is a more robust and generalizable strategy.

Global node enables scaling: Removing the global node (GABAR-G) halves performance on hard problems (42.5% coverage). The global node is critical for rapid information propagation in large graphs.
